Client Spotlight: Swipe, match, scam

I met Drew online and we quickly struck up a romance. We’d even discussed marriage, although we’d never met in person.

One day, Drew called and told me that there had been an accident on the oil rig where he worked. He’d been injured and needed money for medical expenses. So, I asked my financial advisor to wire $20,000 to Drew immediately. He had provided me with his Social Security number and driver’s license, which made me feel more confident that the transaction was legitimate.

While I gathered the information I needed to complete the transfer, I was able to stop for a moment and think it through. I had read that once I sent the money, there’s little that could be done to get it back. Then, I received a phone call from the fraud department and, after answering some questions, I realized that everything was a lie, and I was being scammed.  

I quickly blocked Drew from all communications, thankful that I’d learned a valuable lesson.

Client Spotlight: From fixer-upper to fiasco

I recently visited my local financial center with the intention of paying a contractor for home improvement work. I was going to withdraw $45,000 for the contractor to do the work What helped me avoid what would have been a devastating loss was considering some of the potential red flags like the contractor wanting payment in full, up front and only in cash before any work had been done.

By stopping to think about it, I realized that this was in fact a scam and I was able to save a lot of money and a considerable amount of heartache.

Client Spotlight: “Sure thing” investment gone wrong

I’d held several positions within the banking industry and considered myself well versed on how scams work, so when I had the opportunity to invest money that would give me a large payout to help me repay a loan I’d taken out, I agreed. I started by sending money to an investment firm, after which I would receive a large payout, invest more money, receive another payout, etc. But when the investment amount increased to $200,000, I took a beat — something just didn’t seem right. The wire amounts had been getting larger and larger, and while I’d been receiving payouts, my last return had been late.

It turns out the money I was sending was going to multiple accounts, not to the supposed investment firm. The returns I’d received were sent just to entice me to send larger amounts to invest. I realized I was being scammed, didn’t proceed with the $200,000 transfer and ceased all contact with the scammers.
